Computer Game Design


Once Upon a Time is a 2D vertical scrolling action game that takes a new twist on traditional storytelling by combining it with exciting combat elements and compelling graphics. The gameplay is heavily story driven and is divided into books and chapters. In each chapter, dialog will drive the story forward, however, to complete each chapter and progress in the story you must reach and defeat a boss. To do this one controls a character, moving him/her horizontally to jump on objects in order to climb up towards the platform where the boss is. The type of objects you jump on will vary and depend on the storyline in each chapter. The boss is then defeated by avoiding whatever harmful projectiles he hurls at the player while making sure to get hit by the projectiles that can be reflected back at the boss. The purpose of the game is to stimulate children aged from 8 to 12 to read books that have a lot of visual content, such as fairy tales. In this way, pictures of the books, dialogs, the main characters, and other book elements are present in the game in order to briefly introduce the story and create some interest in the children to read the book after playing the game.

Wizard: A Tangible Tool for Media Navigation


This project aims to rethink traditional forms of media navigation. Primarily, it explores the idea of controlling a media stream using gestures and how a presence of a tangible object (object-mediated gesture) can affect the experience. The project explores affordances of different shapes through participants’ input. An interaction through Leap Motion was also developed. Finally we came up with three prototypes: tangible (crystal ball) controller, non-tangible (no object) controller, and Leap Motion based controller. From these prototypes we assessed the perceived sense of control, the social aspects and the comfort and playfulness of the interactions. The results were mixed but it was discovered that in a social setting, the tangible controller could help people to understand how to participate in the interaction. As we hypothesized, participants felt that the tangible interaction gives them the best sense of control out of the three interactions. We summarize learnings as well future research recommendations including exploring social experience further.
